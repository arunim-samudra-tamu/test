6. Differentials & Linear Approximation

Exercises

    a. Differences & Differentials

    1
  1. Consider the function \(f(x)=x^2\).

    1. Find the tangent line at \(x=3\).

      The equation of the tangent line at \(x=a\) is: \[ y=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a) \]

      \(y=9+6(x-3)\)

      The function is: \[ f(x)=x^2 \] The function value at \(x=3\) is: \[ f(3)=9 \] The derivative is: \[ f'(x)=2x \] At \(x=3\) the derivative is: \[ f'(3)=6 \]

      So the tangent line is: \[\begin{aligned} y&=f(3)+f'(3)(x-3) \\ &=9+6(x-3) \end{aligned}\] The plots of the function and the tangent line are shown.

      sw 

    2. Find the change in \(x\) and the change in the function between \(a=3\) and \(x=3.2\).

      The changes are \[ \Delta x=x-a \]
      \( \Delta f=f(x)-f(a) \)

      \(\Delta x=.2\)
      \(\Delta f=1.24\)

      The change in \( x \) is: \[ \Delta x=x-a=3.2-3=0.2 \] The change in \( f \) is: \[\begin{aligned} \Delta f&=f(x)-f(a)=f(3.2)-f(3) \\ &=3.2^2-3^2=1.24 \end{aligned}\]

      sw 

    3. Find the differential of \(x\) and the differential of the function between \(a=3\) and \(x=3.2\).

      The differential of \( x \) is: \[ dx=x-a \] The differential of \( f(x) \) is: \[ df=f_{\text{tan}}(x)-f(a)=f'(a)\, dx df=f_{\text{tan}}(x)-f(a)=f'(a)\,dx \]

      \( dx=0.2\)
      \( df=1.2 \)

      The differential of x is \[ dx=3.2-3=0.2 \] The differential of f(x) is \[ df=f_{\text{tan}}(3.2)-f(3) =f'(3)\, dx=6\cdot 0.2=1.2 df=f'(3)dx=6\cdot 0.2=1.2 \]

      sw 

      We check by computing the differential by a different method: \[ df=f_{\text{tan}}(3.2)-f(3) =(9+6(3.2-3))-9=1.2 \]

  2. Consider the function \( g(x)=2x^2+3x+5 \).

    1. Find the tangent line at \( x=2 \).

      The tangent line to a function at \( x=a \) is: \[ y=g(a)+g'(a)(x-a) \]

      \( y=19+11(x-2) \)

      The function at \( x=2 \) is: \[ g(2)=2\cdot 2^2+3\cdot 2+5=19 \] The derivative is: \[ g'(x)=4x+3 \] The derivative at \( x=2 \) is: \[ g'(2)=4\cdot 2+3=11 \] The tangent line at \( x=2 \) is: \[ y=g(2)+g'(2)(x-2)=19+11(x-2) \] The plot of the function and the tangent line are shown.

      sw 

    2. Find the change in \( x \) the function between \( a=2\) and \( x=2.1 \).

      The change in \( x \) is: \[ \Delta x=x-a \] The change in \( g \) is: \[ \Delta g=g(x)-g(a) \]

      \( \Delta x=0.1 \)
      \( \Delta g=1.12 \)

      The change in \( x \) is: \[ \Delta x=x-a=2.1-2=0.1 \] Since \(g(2.1)=2(2.1)^2+3(2.1)+5=20.12\), the change in \( g \) is: \[ \begin{aligned} \Delta g &=g(x)-g(a)=g(2.1)-g(2)\\ &=20.12-19=1.12 \end{aligned} \]

      sw 

    3. Find the differential of \( x \) and the differential of the function between \( a=2 \) and \( x=2.1 \).

      The differential of \( x \) is: \[ dx=x-a \] The differential of \( g(x) \) is: \[ dg=g_{\text{tan}}(x)-g(a)=g'(a)\, dx dg=g_{\text{tan}}(x)-g(a)=g'(3)dx \]

      \( dx=0.1 \)
      \( dg=1.1 \)

      The differential of \( x \) is: \[ \, dx=x-a=2.1-2=0.1 \] The differential of \( g(x) \) is: \[ \, dg=g_{\text{tan}}(x)-g(a)=g'(3)\, dx=11\cdot 0.1=1.1 dg=g'(3)dx=11\cdot 0.1=1.1 \]

      sw 

  3. Consider the function \( v(x)=x^3-\dfrac{17}{4x} \).

    1. Find the tangent line at \( x=2 \).

      The tangent line to a function at \( x=a \) is: \[ y=v(a)+v'(a)(x-a) \]

      \( y=5.88+13.06(x-2) \)

      The function at \( x=2 \) is: \[ v(2)=2^3-\frac{17}{4\cdot 2}=\dfrac{47}{8}=5.875 \] The derivative is: \[ v'(x)=3x^2-\left(-\frac{17}{4\cdot x^2}\right)=3x^2+\frac{17}{4x^2} \] The derivative at \( x=2 \) is: \[ v'(2)=3\cdot 2^2+\frac{17}{4\cdot 2^2}=\dfrac{209}{16}\approx13.06 \]

      So the tangent line is: \[\begin{aligned} y&=v(2)+v'(2)(x-2) \\ &=\dfrac{47}{8}+\dfrac{209}{16}(x-2) \end{aligned}\] The plots of the function and the tangent line are shown.

      sw 

    2. Find the change in \( x \) and the change in the function between \( a=2 \) and \( x=2.3 \).

      The change in \( x \) is: \[ \Delta x=x-a \] The change in \( v \) is: \[ \Delta v=v(x)-v(a) \]

      The change in \( x \) is: \[ \Delta x=0.3 \] The change \( v \) is: \[ \Delta v=4.44 \]

      The change in \( x \) is: \[ \Delta x=x-a=2.3-2=0.3 \] Since \(v(2.3)=(2.3)^3+\dfrac{17}{4\cdot2.3}=10.32\), the change in \( v \) is: \[ \Delta v=v(x)-v(a)=v(2.3)-v(2)=10.32-5.88=4.44 \]

      sw 

    3. Find the differential of \( x \) and the differential of the function between \( a=2 \) and \( x=2.3 \).

      The differential of \( x \) is: \[ \, dx=x-a \] The differential of \( v(x) \) is: \[ \, dv=v_{\text{tan}}(x)-v(a)=v'(a)\, dx dv=v_{\text{tan}}(x)-v(a)=v'(2)dx \]

      The differential of \( x \) is: \[ \, dx=0.3 \] The differential of \( v(x) \) is: \[ \, dv=3.76 dv=3.92 \]

      The differential of \( x \) is: \[ \, dx=x-a=2.3-2=0.3 \] The differential of \( v(x) \) is: \[ \, dv=v_{\text{tan}}(x)-v(a)=v'(2)\, dx=12.53\cdot 0.3=3.76 dv=v'(2)\,dx=13.06\cdot 0.3=3.92 \]

      sw 

  4. A spherical ballon of radius \( r=6 \) cm slowly leaks. If the radius of the ballon decreases \( 0.4 \) cm, find the change and differential of the volume of the balloon. (The volume of a sphere is \(\dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3\).)

    \(\Delta V=-169.16\)
    \(dV=-180.96\)

    The old volume was: \[ V_i=\dfrac{4}{3}\pi 6^3=288\pi \] The new volume is: \[ V_f=\dfrac{4}{3}\pi 5.6^3=234.154\overline{6}\pi \] So the change in volume is: \[\begin{aligned} \Delta V&=234.154\overline{6}\pi-288\pi\\ &=-53.845\overline{3}\pi=-169.16 \end{aligned}\] To find the differential we need the derivative:: \[\begin{aligned} V'(r)&=4\pi r^2\\ V'(6)&=4\pi (6)^2=144\pi \end{aligned}\] Then, the differential is: \[\begin{aligned} dV&=V'(6)\,dr =144\pi(-0.4) \\ &=-57.6\pi=-180.96 \end{aligned}\] So the differential is a reasonable approximation to the change. The plot shows the old and new volumes

    sw 

    5. b. Linear Approximation

    1

  5. Use a linear approximation to estimate \( \sqrt[\scriptsize3]{27.4} \).

    Which value \(x\) near \(27.4\) gives a simple value for \(\sqrt[\scriptsize3]{x}\)? Approximate near that value.

    \(\sqrt[\scriptsize3]{27.4}\approx3.0\overline{148}\).

    We take \( f(x)=\sqrt[\scriptsize3]{x} \), since we know \(f(27)=3\). We then construct the tangent function. We have: \[\begin{aligned} f'(x)&=\dfrac{1}{3\sqrt[\scriptsize3]{x^2}} \\ f'(27)&=\dfrac{1}{3\sqrt[\scriptsize3]{27^2}}=\dfrac{1}{27} \end{aligned}\] So the tangent function is: \[ f_{\text{tan}}(x)=f(27)+f'(27)(x-27)=3+\dfrac{1}{27}(x-27) \] Finally, let \( x=27.4 \). Then: \[\begin{aligned} \sqrt[\scriptsize3]{27.4}&=f(27.4)\approx f_{\text{tan}}(27.4) \\ &=3+\dfrac{1}{27}(27.4-27) \\ &=3+\dfrac{0.4}{27}=3.0\overline{148} \end{aligned}\]

    sw 

    The actual value of \( \sqrt[\scriptsize3]{27.4} \) is \( 3.01474 \). Our approximation is \(3.0\overline{148}\). Since these are so close our approximation is an accurate one.

  6. Use a linear approximation to estimate \( \dfrac{1}{\sqrt[\scriptsize4]{16.3}} \).

    Which value \(x\) near \(16.3\) gives a simple value for \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt[\scriptsize4]{x}}\)? Approximate near that value.

    \( \dfrac{1}{\sqrt[\scriptsize4]{16.3}}\approx0.49766\).

    We take \( f(x)=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt[\scriptsize4]{x}} \), since we know \(f(16)=\dfrac{1}{2}\). We then construct the tangent function. We have: \[\begin{aligned} f'(x)&=-\dfrac{1}{4\sqrt[\scriptsize4]{x^5}} \\ f'(16)&=-\dfrac{1}{4\sqrt[\scriptsize4]{16^5}}=-\dfrac{1}{128} \end{aligned}\] So the tangent function is: \[ f_{\text{tan}}(x)=f(16)+f'(16)(x-16)=\dfrac{1}{2}-\dfrac{1}{128}(x-16) \] Finally, let \( x=16.3 \). Then: \[\begin{aligned} \dfrac{1}{\sqrt[\scriptsize4]{16.3}}&=f(16.3)\approx f_{\text{tan}}(16.3) \\ &=\dfrac{1}{2}-\dfrac{1}{128}(16.3-16) \\ &=\dfrac{1}{2}-\dfrac{0.3}{128}=0.49766 \end{aligned}\]

    sw 

    The actual value of \(\dfrac{1}{\sqrt[\scriptsize4]{16.3}}\) is \( 0.49768 \). Our approximation was \(0.49766\) , since these are so close our approximation is an accurate one.

  7. A conical cup is filled with water to a height \( h=9 \) cm and radius \( r=2 \) cm, but it is leaking. If \( 0.5 \) cubic cm leaks out, estimate the change in the height of the water. (The volume of a cone is \(V=\dfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2h\).)

    Implicitly differentiate the volume equation.

    \(\Delta h\approx0.0398\).

    Since \(\dfrac{h}{r}=\dfrac{9}{2}\), we can write \(r\) as \(r=\dfrac{2}{9}h\). Plugging that into the volume of the cone we get: \[ V=\dfrac{1}{3}\pi \left(\dfrac{2}{9}h\right)^2h =\dfrac{4}{243}\pi h^3 \] To get the change in the height, we first approximate the change in the volume using differentials: \[ \Delta V\approx dV=\dfrac{dV}{dh}\,dh\approx\dfrac{4}{81}\pi h^2\Delta h \] We now solve for \(\Delta h\): \[ \Delta h\approx\dfrac{81}{4\pi h^2}\Delta V =\dfrac{81}{4\pi (9)^2}0.5=\dfrac{1}{8\pi} =0.0398 \]

    sw 

    The initial volume is \[ V_i=\dfrac{4}{243}\pi (9)^3=12\pi \] The final volume is \[ V_f=12\pi-.5 \] The initial height is \[ h_i=9 \] The final height is \[ h_f=\left(\dfrac{243(12\pi-.5)}{4\pi}\right)^{1/3} =8.9601 \] The actual change in height is: \[ \Delta h=9-8.9601=.0399 \] Our estimate is: \[ \Delta h\approx0.0398 \] Since these are so close our estimate is an accurate one.

  8. A little girl is pouring sand from a bucket forming a cone whose radius is always twice it height. Currently, the radius is \(r=4\) cm. If she pours \(5\) cm\(^3\) more sand on the pile, how much does the radius change? (The volume of a cone is \(V=\dfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2h\).)

    Express the volume in terms of \(r\) and differentiate. Then find the differential of the volume.

    \(\Delta r=\dfrac{5}{8\pi}\approx0.199\)

    Since the radius is always twice the height, we have \(r=2h\) or \(h=\dfrac{r}{2}\). We can write the volume of the sand as: \[ V=\dfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2h =\dfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2\dfrac{r}{2} =\dfrac{1}{6}\pi r^3 \] So \(\dfrac{dV}{dr}=\dfrac{1}{2}\pi r^2\). To get the change in the radius, we compute the change in the volume using differentials: \[ \Delta V\approx dV=\dfrac{dV}{dr}\,dr\approx \dfrac{1}{2}\pi r^2\Delta r \] We now solve for \(\Delta r\): \[ \Delta r\approx\dfrac{2}{\pi r^2}\Delta V =\dfrac{2}{\pi (4)^2}5=\dfrac{5}{8\pi} \approx0.199 \]

    sw 

    The initial volume is \[ V_i=\dfrac{1}{12}\pi (4)^3=16.76 \] The final volume is \[ V_f=16.767+5=21.76 \] The initial radius is \[ r_i=4 \] The final radius is \[ r_f=\sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{\dfrac{12V_f}{\pi}} =\sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{\dfrac{12(21.76)}{\pi}} \approx 4.364 \] So the actual change in radius is: \[ \Delta r=4.364-4=0.364 \] Our estimate is: \[ \Delta r\approx0.398 \] Since these are close our estimate is reasonable.

    9. c. Mean Value Theorem

    1

  9. Consider the function \(f(x)=x^3\) on the interval \([-3,3]\). Find the number(s) \(c\in (-3,3)\) which are guaranteed to exist by the MVT.

    Find where the derivative is equal to the slope between the endpoints.

    \(c=\sqrt{3},-\sqrt{3}\)

    The values at the endpoints are: \[ f(-3)=-27 \qquad f(3)=27 \] The slope between the endpoints is: \[ m=\dfrac{f(3)-f(-3)}{3-(-3)}=\dfrac{27-(-27)}{6}=9 \] The derivative is: \[ f'(x)=3x^2 \] So the Mean Value Theorem guarantees there is a number \(c\in(-3,3)\) where: \[ f'(c)=3c^2=9 \] So \(c=\sqrt{3},-\sqrt{3}\).

    sw 

    We check by evaluating the derivative \(f'(x)=3x^2\) at \(c=\sqrt{3}\) and \(c=-\sqrt{3}\): \[\begin{aligned} f'(\sqrt{3})&=3(\sqrt{3})^2=9 \\ f'(-\sqrt{3})&=3(-\sqrt{3})^2=9 \end{aligned}\] Here is a plot of the function, the line between the endpoints and the tangent lines at \(c=\sqrt{3}\) and \(c=-\sqrt{3}\). Notice the tangent lines are parallel to the line between the endpoints.

  10. Consider the function \(f(x)=2x^3+3x\) on the interval \([-2,4]\). Find the number(s) \(c\in (-2,4)\) which are guaranteed to exist by the MVT.

    Find where the derivative is equal to the slope between the endpoints.

    \(c=2,-2\)

    The values at the endpoints are: \[ f(-2)=-16-6=-22 \qquad f(4)=128+12=140 \] The slope between the endpoints is: \[ m=\dfrac{f(4)-f(-2)}{4-(-2)}=\dfrac{140-(-22)}{6}=27 \] The derivative is: \[ f'(x)=6x^2+3 \] So the Mean Value Theorem guarantees there is a number \(c\in(-2,4)\) where: \[ f'(c)=6c^2+3=27 \] or \(6c^2=24\) or \(c=2,-2\).

    sw 

    We check by evaluating the derivative \(f'(x)=6x^2+3\) at \(c=2\) and \(c=-2\): \[ f'(2)=6(2)^2+3=27 \qquad f'(-2)=6(-2)^2+3=27 \] Here is a plot of the function, the line between the endpoints and the tangent lines at \(c=2\) and \(c=-2\). Notice the tangent lines are parallel to the line between the endpoints.

  11. Consider the function \( g(x)=x^2+2x \) on the interval \( [-1,1] \). Find the number(s) \(c\in (-1,1)\) which are guaranteed to exist by the MVT.

    Find where the derivative is equal to the slope between the endpoints.

    \(c=0\)

    The values at the endpoints are: \[ g(-1)=-1 \qquad g(1)=3 \] The slope between the endpoints is: \[ m=\dfrac{g(1)-g(-1)}{1-(-1))}=\dfrac{3-(-1)}{2}=2 \] The derivative is: \[ g'(x)=2x+2 \] So the Mean Value Theorem guarantees there is a number \(c\in (-1,1)\) where: \[ g'(c)=2c+2=2 \] So \(c=0\)

    sw 

    We check by evaluating the derivative \(g'(x)=2x+2\) at \(c=0\): \[ g'(0)=2(0)+2=2 \] Here is a plot of the function, the line between the endpoints and the tangent line at \(c=0\). Notice the tangent line is parallel to the line between the endpoints.

  12. Consider the function \(v(x)=4x^3+6x^2-12x\) on the interval \((-6,6)\). Find the number(s) which are guaranteed to exist by the MVT.

    Find where the derivative is equal to the slope between the endpoints.

    \(c=-4,3\)

    The values at the endpoints are: \[\begin{aligned} f(-6)&=-4(216)+6(36)+12(6)=-864+216+72=-576 \\ f(6)&=4(216)+6(36)-12(6)=864+216-72=1008 \end{aligned}\] The slope between the endpoints is: \[ m=\dfrac{v(6)-v(-6)}{6-(-6)}=\dfrac{1008-(-576)}{12}=132 \] The derivative is: \[ v'(x)=12x^2+12x-12 \] So the Mean Value Theorem guarantees there is a number \( c\in (-6,6) \) where: \[ v'(c)=12c^2+12c-12=132 \] If we factor we get: \[ 0=12c^2+12c-144=12(c+4)(c-3) \] So \(c=-4,3\).

    sw 

    We check by evaluating the derivative \(v'(x)=12c^2+12c-12\) at \(c=-4\) and \(c=3\): \[\begin{aligned} v'(-4)&=12(16)+12(-4)-12=132 \\ v'(3)&=12(9)+12(3)-12=132 \end{aligned}\] Here is a plot of the function, the line between the endpoints and the tangent lines at \(c=-4\) and \(c=3\). Notice the tangent lines are parallel to the line between the endpoints.
