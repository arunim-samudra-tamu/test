26. Stokes' Theorem

Let \(S\) be a nice surface in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) with a nice properly oriented boundary, \(\partial S\), and let \(\vec{F}\) be a nice vector field on \(S\). Then \[ \iint_S \vec{\nabla}\times\vec{F}\cdot d\vec{S} =\oint_{\partial S} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s} \] Each piece of the boundary of the surface must be traversed counterclockwise as seen from the tip of the normal vector to the surface.

d. Applications

6. Which Surface Integrals Can be Done Using Stokes' Theorem?

THIS IS FROM FTCC

Suppose you are asked to compute \(\displaystyle \underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_A^B} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}\). Can you always use the FTCC to do this? No! If \(\vec{F}\) has a scalar potential, \(f\), then \(\vec{F}=\vec{\nabla}f\) and by the FTCC: \[ \underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_A^B} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s} =\underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_A^B} \vec{\nabla}f\cdot d\vec{s} =f(B)-f(A) \] However, if \(\vec{F}\) does not have a scalar potential, then you can not use the FTCC and you must do the integral directly as: \[ \underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_A^B} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s} =\int_a^b \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t))\cdot\vec{v}\,dt \] See the basic definition of a line integral of a vector.

Compute \(\displaystyle \underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_{(1,1,1)}^{(2,4,8)}} \vec F\cdot d\vec{s}\) along the curve \(\vec r(t)=(t,t^2,t^3)\) for the vector field \(\vec F=\langle yz,xz,xy\rangle\).

We first note that a scalar potential for \(\vec F\) is \(f=xyz\). Then we simply use the theorem: \[\begin{aligned} \underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_{(1,1,1)}^{(2,4,8)}} \vec F\cdot d\vec{s} &=\underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_{(1,1,1)}^{(2,4,8)}} \vec\nabla f\cdot d\vec{s} \\ &=f(2,4,8)-f(1,1,1) \\ &=2\cdot4\cdot8-1\cdot1\cdot1=63 \end{aligned}\]

Compute \(\displaystyle \underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_{(1,1,1)}^{(2,4,8)}} \vec F\cdot d\vec{s}\) along the curve \(\vec r(t)=(t,t^2,t^3)\) for the vector field \(\vec F=\langle 3yz,2xz,xy\rangle\).

We first note that there is no scalar potential for \(\vec F\) because its curl is non-zero: \[\begin{aligned} \vec\nabla\times\vec F &=\begin{vmatrix} \hat{\imath} & \hat{\jmath} & \hat{k} \\ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z \\ 3yz & 2xz & xy \end{vmatrix} \\ &=\hat{\imath}(x-2x)-\hat{\jmath}(y-3y)+\hat{k}(2z-3z) \\ &=\langle -x,2y,-z\rangle \ne \vec{0} \end{aligned}\] So we need to compute the integral directly. The value of the vector field on the curve is: \[ \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t))=\langle 3(t^2)(t^3),2(t)(t^3),(t)(t^2)\rangle =\langle 3t^5,2t^4,t^3\rangle \] The velocity of the curve is: \[ \vec v=\langle 1,2t,3t^2\rangle \] And we find the values of \(t\) at the endpoints: \[\begin{aligned} (t,t^2,t^3)&=(1,1,1) \quad \text{at} \quad t=1 \\ (t,t^2,t^3)&=(2,4,8) \quad \text{at} \quad t=2 \end{aligned}\] So the integral is: \[\begin{aligned} \underset{\vec{r}(t)\quad}{\int_{(1,1,1)}^{(2,4,8)}} &\vec F\cdot d\vec{s} =\int_1^2 \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t))\cdot\vec{v}\,dt \\ &=\int_1^2 (3t^5\cdot1+2t^4\cdot2t+t^3\cdot3t^2)\,dt \\ &=\int_1^2 10t^5\,dt =\left[\rule{0pt}{10pt}2t^6\right]_1^2 =126 \end{aligned}\]

Calculate the integral \(\displaystyle \int_{\vec{r}(t)} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}\) where \(\vec{F}=\langle yz,x^2z,xy\rangle\) along the curve \(\vec{r}(t)=(3,2t,t^2)\) between \(t=1\) and \(t=3\).

\(\displaystyle \int_{\vec{r}(t)} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}=260\)

We first try to find a scalar potential. To solve \(\vec{\nabla}f=\vec{F}\), we need to solve: \[ \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}=yz, \qquad \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}=x^2z \qquad \text{and} \qquad \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial z}=xy \] From the first of these, \(\displaystyle f=\int yz\,dx=xyz+g(y,z)\). Plugging into the second gives: \[ \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y} =\partial_y(xyz+g(y,z)) =xz+\dfrac{\partial g}{\partial y} =x^2z \quad \text{or} \quad \dfrac{\partial g}{\partial y}=x^2z-xz \] But \(g\) is only a function of \(y\) and \(z\). So it's derivative cannot depend on \(x\). This is a contradiction. So no scalar potential exists. Alternatively, we can compute the curl of \(\vec{F}\): \[\begin{aligned} \vec\nabla\times\vec{F} &=\begin{vmatrix} \hat{\imath} & \hat{\jmath} & \hat{k} \\ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z \\ yz & x^2z & xy \end{vmatrix} =\hat{\imath}(x-x^2)-\hat{\jmath}(y-y)+\hat{k}(2xz-z) \\ &=\langle x-x^2,0,2xz-z\rangle \ne \vec{0} \end{aligned}\] Since the curl is non-zero, there is no scalar potential and we have to compute the line integral directly.

We evaluate \(\vec{F}\) along the curve \(\vec{r}(t)\) and find the velocity vector: \[ \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) =\langle 2t^3,9t^2, 6t\rangle \qquad \qquad \vec{v}=\langle0,2,2t\rangle \] \[\begin{aligned} \int_{\vec{r}(t)} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s} &=\int_1^3 \langle 2t^3,9t^2,6t\rangle\cdot\langle 0,2,2t\rangle\,dt =\int_1^3 (18t^2+12t^2)\,dt\\ &=\int_1^3 30t^2\,dt =10\left[\rule{0pt}{10pt}t^3\right]_1^3 =260 \end{aligned}\]

Calculate the integral \(\displaystyle \int_{\vec{r}(t)} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}\) where \(\vec{F}=\langle 2x,2y,2z\rangle\) along the curve \(\vec{r}(t)=(t^3,t^2,t)\) between \(t=1\) and \(t=2\).

\(\displaystyle \int_{\vec{r}(t)} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s}=81\)

By inspection, a scalar potential for \(\vec{F}=\langle 2x,2y,2z\rangle\) is: \[ f=x^2+y^2+z^2 \] The endpoints are \[ A=\vec r(1)=(1,1,1) \qquad \text{and} \qquad B=\vec r(2)=(8,4,2) \] So the integral is: \[\begin{aligned} \int_{\vec{r}(t)} \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{s} &=f(B)-f(A) \\ &=(8^2+4^2+2^2)-(1^2+1^2+1^2)=81 \end{aligned}\]

Notice that when you can use the FTCC, the integral is much easier to compute.
