  4. Integrate the function \(\displaystyle f=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) over the region between \(y=x\), \(x=0\) and \(y=\sqrt{9-x^2}\)

    xxx

    \(\displaystyle \int \int f(r,\theta)\,dA=\dfrac{9}{4}\pi\)

    In polar coordinates the region is \(\dfrac{\pi}{4} < \theta < \dfrac{\pi}{2}\) and \(0 < r < 3\). The integrand becomes \(f=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=r\) and the area differential is \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\) so we can set up the integral as: \[\begin{aligned} \iint r\,dA &=\int_{\pi/4}^{\pi/2}\int_0^3 r^2\,dr\,d\theta \\ &=\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}-\,\dfrac{\pi}{4}\right) \left[\dfrac{1}{3}r^3\right]_0^3 =\dfrac{9}{4}\pi \end{aligned}\]

  5. Find the area of the cardiod \(r=1+\cos\theta\) by integrating in the polar coordinate system.

    Set up the integral as \[ \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{1+\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta \]

    \[ \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{1+\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta=\dfrac{3 }{2}\pi \]

    We can write the area as the integral \[ A=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{1+\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta \] Doing the inner integral: \[ \int_0^{1+\cos\theta} r\,dr =\dfrac{1}{2}(\cos\theta+1)^2 \] Now doing the outer integral: \[\begin{aligned} A&=\int_0^{2\pi} \dfrac{1}{2}(\cos\theta+1)^2\,d\theta \\ &=\dfrac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(\cos^2\theta+2\cos\theta+1) \,d\theta \\ &=\dfrac{1}{2}\left[\dfrac{1}{2}(\theta+\sin\theta\cos\theta)+2\sin\theta+\theta\right]_0^{2\pi} \\ &=\dfrac{3}{2}\pi \end{aligned}\]

  6. Find the area of the circle \(r=\cos\theta\) by integrating in the polar coordinate system.

    When you plot the curve the circle lies only in quadrants I and IV. So you don't integrate from \(0\) to \(2\pi\).

    \( A=\dfrac{\pi}{4} \)

    \[ A=\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta =\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} \dfrac{1}{2}\cos^2\theta\,d\theta=\dfrac{\pi}{4} \]

  7. Find the area outside of the circle \(r=1\) and inside the cardioid \(r=2-2\cos\theta\). The curves are plotted with the region of interest in blue.

    In this problem the bounds of integration go from \(r=1\) to \(r=2-2\cos\theta\) (because the circle is the minimum value of \(r\) and the cardioid is the maximum value of \(r\)) while the bounds of \(\theta\) must be calculated from the intersection of the circle and the cardioid.

    \[ A=\int_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3}\int_1^{2-2\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta =\dfrac{10}{3}\pi+\dfrac{7}{2}\sqrt{3} \]

    In polar coordinates, \(r\) ranges from \(1\) to \(2-2\cos\theta\). The \(\theta\) bounds must be calculated from the intersection of the circle and the cardioid. We do this by setting \[ r=1=2-2\cos\theta \] So \[ 2\cos\theta=1 \qquad \text{or} \qquad \cos\theta=\dfrac{1}{2} \qquad \text{or} \qquad \theta=\dfrac{\pi}{3}, \dfrac{5\pi}{3} \] Now that we have the limits we can set up the area integral: \[\begin{aligned} A&=\int_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3}\int_1^{2-2\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta =\dfrac{10}{3}\pi+\dfrac{7}{2}\sqrt{3} =\int_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3} \dfrac{1}{2}((2-2\cos\theta)^2-1^2)\,d\theta \\ &=\dfrac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3} (4\cos^2\theta-8\cos\theta+4-1)\,d\theta =\dfrac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3} (4\cos{2}\theta-8\cos\theta+3)\,d\theta \\ &=\dfrac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3} (4\cos^2\theta-8\cos\theta+3)\,d\theta =\dfrac{1}{2}\left[2(\theta+\sin\theta\cos\theta)-8\sin\theta+3\theta\dfrac{}{}\right]_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3} \\ &=\dfrac{1}{2} 2\left[(\theta+\sin\theta\cos\theta)\dfrac{}{}\right]_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3} +\dfrac{1}{2}\left[-8\sin\theta+3\theta\dfrac{}{}\right]_{\pi/3}^{5\pi/3} \\ &=\left(\dfrac{5\pi}{3}+\sin\dfrac{5\pi}{3}\cos\dfrac{5\pi}{3} -\,\dfrac{\pi}{3}-\sin\dfrac{\pi}{3}\cos\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right) +\dfrac{1}{2}\left(-8\sin\dfrac{5\pi}{3}+3\dfrac{5\pi}{3} -\left(-8\sin\dfrac{\pi}{3}+3\dfrac{\pi}{3}\right) \right) \\ &=\dfrac{4\pi}{3}-\,\dfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{3}+2\pi+4\sqrt{3} \\ &=\dfrac{10}{3}\pi+\dfrac{7}{2}\sqrt{3} \end{aligned}\]

  9. Compute the integral \(\displaystyle \int\int\int f(x,y,z)\,dV\) over the cylindrical region with \(0 \le z \le 3\), centered on the \(z\)-axis having a diameter of \(4\) with the integrand \(f(x,y,z)=x^2\).

    Convert \(f(x)=x^2\) to cylindrical coordinates by: \[ x=r\cos\theta \] The diameter is 4, therefore the radius is 2: this should be the upper bound on the \(dr\) integral. The \(dz\) integral should go from \(0\) to \(3\). The integral is over the entire cylindrical region, therefore the \(d\theta\) integral ranges from \(0\) to \(2\pi\) Remember \(dV=r\,dr\,dz\,d\theta\)

    \[ \int_0^3\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^3\cos^2\theta\,dr\,d\theta\,dz=12\pi \]

    The integral can be set up as: \[ \iiint r^2\cos^2\theta\,dV =\int_0^3\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^3\cos^2\theta\,dr\,d\theta\,dz \] We can now factor as follows: \[\begin{aligned} \int_0^3\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^3\cos^2\theta\,dr\,d\theta\,dz &=\int_0^3 \,dz\int_0^{2\pi} \cos^2\theta\,d\theta\int_0^2 r^3\,dr =3\int_0^{2\pi} \cos^2\theta\,d\theta \dfrac{1}{4}\left[r^4\dfrac{}{}\right]_0^2 \\ &=12\int_0^{2\pi} \dfrac{1+\cos(2\theta) }{2}\,d\theta=12 \left[\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\theta+\dfrac{\sin(2\theta) }{2} \right) \right]_0^{2\pi} \\ &=12\pi \end{aligned}\]

  10. Compute the integral \(\displaystyle \int\int\int x^2+y^2\,dV\) over the cylindrical region with the base centered at the origin having a diameter of \(8\) and a height of \(0 \le z \le 2\).

    xxx

    \[ \int_0^2\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^4 r^3\,dr\,d\theta\,dz=256\pi \]

    The integral can be computed as \[\begin{aligned} \int_0^2\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^4 r^3\,dr\,d\theta\,dz &=\int_0^2 \,dz\int_0^{2\pi} \,d\theta\int_0^4 r^3\,dr \\ &=2\cdot2\pi\left[\dfrac{1}{4}r^4\right]_0^4 \\ &=4\pi\left(\dfrac{4^4}{4}\right) \\ &=256\pi \end{aligned}\]

  11. The solid at the right was made by revolving the curve \(x=4-z^2\) around the \(z\)-axis. So in cylindrical coordinates, it is \(r=4-z^2\) independent of \(\theta\).
    PY: Checked

    1. Find the volume.

      \(V=\dfrac{512}{15}\pi\)

      Since \(r=4-z^2\) is a function of \(z\), we put the \(r\)-integral inside. The volume is: \[\begin{aligned} V&=\iiint 1\,dV =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_{-2}^2\int_0^{4-z^2} r\,dr\,dz\,d\theta \\ &=2\pi\int_{-2}^2 \left[\dfrac{r^2}{2}\right]_0^{4-z^2}\,dz =\pi\int_{-2}^2 (4-z^2)^2\,dz \\ &=\pi\int_{-2}^2 (16-8z^2+z^4)\,dz =\pi\left[16z-8\,\dfrac{z^3}{3}+\dfrac{z^5}{5}\right]_{-2}^2 \\ &=2\pi\left(32-\,\dfrac{64}{3}+\dfrac{32}{5}\right) =64\pi\left(1-\,\dfrac{2}{3}+\dfrac{1}{5}\right) =\dfrac{512}{15}\pi \end{aligned}\]

      This shape is contained in a cylinder of radius \(r=4\) and height \(h=4\) whose volume is \(V=\pi r^2h=64\pi\). So having a volume \(V=\dfrac{512}{15}\pi\approx32.1\pi\) is reasonable.

    2. Find the mass if the density is \(\delta=r\).

      \(M=\dfrac{8192}{105}\pi\)

      Since the density is \(\delta=r\) the mass is: \[\begin{aligned} M&=\iiint \delta\,dV =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_{-2}^2\int_0^{4-z^2} r^2\,dr\,dz\,d\theta \\ &=2\pi\int_{-2}^2 \left[\dfrac{r^3}{3}\right]_0^{4-z^2}\,dz =\dfrac{2}{3}\pi\int_{-2}^2 (4-z^2)^3\,dz \\ &=\dfrac{2}{3}\pi\int_{-2}^2 (64-48z^2+12z^4-z^6)\,dz \\ &=\dfrac{2}{3}\pi\left[64z-16z^3+12\dfrac{z^5}{5}-\,\dfrac{z^7}{7}\right]_{-2}^2 \\ &=\dfrac{4}{3}\pi\left(128-128+12\dfrac{32}{5}-\,\dfrac{128}{7}\right) \\ &=\dfrac{512}{3}\pi\left(1-1+\dfrac{3}{5}-\,\dfrac{1}{7}\right) =\dfrac{8192}{105}\pi \end{aligned}\]

    3. Find the average density if the density is \(\delta=r\).

      \(\delta_\text{ave}=\dfrac{16}{7}\)

      The average density is: \[ \delta_\text{ave}=\dfrac{1}{V}\iiint \delta\,dV \] We already computed the volume is \(V=\dfrac{512}{15}\pi\). The numerator is just the mass \(M=\dfrac{8192}{105}\pi\). So the average \[ \delta_\text{ave}=\dfrac{M}{V} =\dfrac{8192\pi}{105}\dfrac{15}{512\pi} =\dfrac{16}{7} \]

      The largest the radius can be is \(r=4\). So the largest the density can be is \(\delta=4\). So having an average density of \(\delta_\text{ave}=\dfrac{16}{7}\approx2.3\) is reasonable.

  12. Find the centroid of the the region beneath the cone \(z=4-r\) and above the \(xy\) plane.
    PY: Checked

    xxx

    \(\left\langle \bar{x},\bar{y},\bar{z}\right\rangle =\left\langle 0,0,1\right\rangle\)

    The \(x\) and \(y\) components of the centroid are both zero due to the symmetry of the object about the \(z\) axis, as you can easily see in the plot. The \(z\) component of the centroid of the cone, however must be computed. The formula for \(\bar{z}\) is: \[ \bar{z}=\dfrac{1}{V}\iiint_R z\,r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz \] You should remember the formula for the volume of a cone is \[\begin{aligned} V&=\dfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2h \\ &=\dfrac{1}{3}\pi(4)^2(4)=\dfrac{64}{3}\pi \end{aligned}\] However it is useful to recompute it to identify the correct structure of the integral: \[\begin{aligned} V&=\iiint_R r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^4\int_0^{4-r} r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta \\ &=\int_0^{2\pi}\,d\theta\int_0^4 \left[zr\dfrac{}{}\right]_{z=0}^{4-r}\,\,dr =2\pi\int_0^4 (4-r)r\,dr \\ &=2\pi\left[2r^2-\,\dfrac{r^3}{3}\right]_0^4 \\ &=\dfrac{64}{3}\pi \end{aligned}\] Next we compute the \(z\)-moment of the volume as \[\begin{aligned} V_z&=\iiint_R z\,r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^4\int_0^{4-r} z\,r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta \\ &=\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\int_0^4 \left[\dfrac{z^2}{2}r\right]_{z=0}^{4-r}\,\,dr =2\pi\dfrac{1}{2}\int_0^4 (4-r)^2r\,dr \\ &=\pi\int_0^4 (16r-8r^2+r^3)\,dr =\pi\left[8r^2-\,\dfrac{8}{3}r^3+\dfrac{1}{4}r^4\right]_0^4 \\ &=\pi\left(8(4)^2-\,\dfrac{8}{3}(4)^3+\dfrac{1}{4}(4)^4\right) =\dfrac{64}{3}\pi \end{aligned}\] Thus the \(z\)-component of the centroid is \[ \bar{z}=\dfrac{V_z}{V}=\dfrac{64\pi}{3}\dfrac{3}{64\pi}=1 \] and so the centroid is \[ \left\langle \bar{x},\bar{y},\bar{z}\right\rangle =\left\langle 0,0,1\right\rangle \]

    Notice that \(\bar{z}=1=\dfrac{h}{4}\). This result can be generalized for the centroid of any cone with its base on the \(xy\) plane with a height \(h\): \[ \left\langle \bar{x},\bar{y},\bar{z}\right\rangle =\left\langle 0,0,\dfrac{1}{4}h\right\rangle \]

  13. Find the 4-Volume below the function \(w=zr^2\) and above the \(w=0\) hyperplane within the region between the cone \(z=4-r\) and the \(xy\) plane.
    PY: Checked

    \(V_4=\dfrac{1024}{15}\pi\)

    The \(4\)-Volume is the triple integral: \[\begin{aligned} V_4&=\iiint zr^2\,dV =\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^4\int_0^{4-r} zr^2\,r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta \\ &=\int_0^{2\pi} \,d\theta\int_0^4 \left[\dfrac{z^2}{2}r^3\right]_{z=0}^{4-r}\,dr =\dfrac{2\pi}{2}\int_0^4 (4-r)^2r^3\,dr \\ &=\pi\int_0^4 r^5-8r^4+16r^3\,dr =\pi\left[\dfrac{1}{6}r^6-\,\dfrac{8}{5}r^5+4r^4\right]_0^4 \\ &=\pi\left(\dfrac{1}{6}(4)^6-\,\dfrac{8}{5}(4)^5+4(4)^4\right) =\dfrac{1024}{15}\pi \end{aligned}\]

  17. Compute the integral \(\displaystyle \iiint\dfrac{1}{z}\,dV\) over the ice cream cone \(0 \le \rho \le 5\), and \(0 \le \phi \le \dfrac{\pi}{4}\).

  19. Find the mass of an apple given in spherical coordinates by \(\rho =1-\cos\phi\) if the mass density is \(\delta=2\) gm/cm\(^3\)

  20. Compute \(\displaystyle I=\int\int_R y^2\,dx\,dy\) where \(R\) is defined as the region bounded by \(y=\dfrac{1}{x},y=\dfrac{4}{x},y=x,\) and \(y=9x\).

    xxx

    \[ I=30 \]

    We first need to define a coordinate system using \(u\) and \(v\). Rearranging the first two boundaries, we find \[ xy=1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad xy=4 \] We can use \(u=xy\) where \(u\) ranges from \(1\) to \(4\). The last two boundaries can be rearranged to \[ \dfrac{y}{x}=1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \dfrac{y}{x}=9 \] So we let \(v=\dfrac{y}{x}\) where \(v\) ranges from \(1\) to \(9\). Since we know \(u=xy\) and \(v=y/x\) we solve \(u,v\) in terms of \(x,y\) and compute the Jacobian: \[\begin{aligned} x&=\dfrac{u}{y},y=xv\rightarrow x=\dfrac{u}{xv}\rightarrow x^2=\dfrac{u}{ v} \\ &\rightarrow &x=\sqrt{\dfrac{u}{v}}=u^{1/2}v^{-1/2} \end{aligned}\] \[\begin{aligned} y&=xv=u^{1/2}v^{-1/2}v \\ &\rightarrow&y=u^{1/2}v^{1/2}=\sqrt{uv} \end{aligned}\] \[ \vec{e}_u =\left\langle \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial u},\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial u}\right\rangle =\left\langle \dfrac{1}{2}u^{-1/2}v^{-1/2},\dfrac{1}{2}u^{-1/2}v^{1/2}\right\rangle \] \[ \vec{e}_v =\left\langle \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial v},\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial v}\right\rangle =\left\langle -\,\dfrac{1}{2}u^{1/2}v^{-3/2},\dfrac{1}{2}u^{1/2}v^{-1/2}\right\rangle \] \[ J=\dfrac{\partial (x,y) }{\partial (u,v) } =\left(\dfrac{1}{4v}+\dfrac{1}{4v}\right)=\dfrac{1}{2v} \] Finally, we solve the integral \[\begin{aligned} I&=\iint_R y^2\,dx\,dy =\int_1^4\int_1^9 y(u,v)^2J\,dv\,du \\ &=\int_1^4\int_1^9 uv\dfrac{1}{2v}\,dv\,du=\dfrac{1}{2} \int_1^4\int_1^9 u\,dv\,du \\ &=\dfrac{1}{2}\left[\dfrac{u^2}{2}\right]_1^4\left[v\dfrac{}{}\right]_1^9 =\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{16-1}{2}\right) (8)=30 \end{aligned}\]

  21. Compute the area of the ellipse \(4x^2+16y^2=36\)

    Use the substitutions \(x=3t\cos\phi\) and \(y=\dfrac{3}{2}t\sin\phi\) Check your solution with the formula for area of an ellipse: \(A=\pi ab\) where \(a,b\) are the major and minor radii

    \[ A=\dfrac{9}{2}\pi \]

    Using the substitutions \(x=3t\cos\phi\) and \(y=\dfrac{3}{2}t\sin\phi\) we find the Jacobian determinant: \[ \vec{e}_t=\left(3\cos\phi,\dfrac{3}{2}\sin\phi\right) \] \[ \vec{e}_\phi=\left(-3t\sin\phi,\dfrac{3}{2}t\cos\phi\right) \] \[ J=\dfrac{\partial (x,y) }{\partial (t,\phi) } =\left(\dfrac{9}{2}t\cos^2\phi+\dfrac{9}{2}t\sin^2\phi\right) =\dfrac{9}{2}t \] Now we set up the integral: \[ A=\iint J\,dt\,d\phi=\iint \dfrac{9}{2}t\,dt\,d\phi \] To find the \(t\)-bounds, we know that \(t=0\) is our starting point, and plugging our substitutions into the ellipse equation: \[ 4(9t^2\cos^2\phi)+16(\dfrac{9}{4}t^2\sin^2\phi)=36 \] \[ 36t^2\cos^2\phi+36t^2\sin^2\phi=36\rightarrow 36t^2=36 \] \[ \rightarrow t^2=1\rightarrow t=1 \] This is our outer \(t\)-bound \(\phi\) goes all the way around the ellipse, from \(0\) to \(2\pi\) Finally we can compute the integral: \[\begin{aligned} A&=\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 \dfrac{9}{2}t\,dt\,d\phi =2\pi\dfrac{9}{4}\left[t^2\dfrac{}{}\right]_0^1 \\ &=2\pi\left(\dfrac{9}{4}\right)=\dfrac{9}{2}\pi \end{aligned}\] Alternatively, the substitutions \(x=4u\) and \(y=2v\) could be used, followed to a change to polar coordinates. The answer will be the same. We can check our answer by the formula for area of an ellipse: \(A=\pi ab\) where \(a,b\) are the major and minor radii. \(a=3\) and \(b=\dfrac{3}{2}\) in this case, so \(A=\pi(3)\left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)=\dfrac{9}{2}\pi\).

    2. Paraboloidal:
      Here are the transformation equations and a plot of its coordinate surfaces: \[ x=uv\cos\theta \qquad y=uv\sin\theta \qquad z=\dfrac{1}{2}(v^2-u^2) \]

      xxx

      \[ J=-uv(u^2+v^2) \]

      To find the Jacobian we need to find \[\begin{aligned} J&=\dfrac{\partial (x,y,z)}{\partial (u,v,\theta)}=\det \begin{vmatrix} \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial v} & \dfrac{ \partial x}{\partial \theta} \\[6pt] \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial v} & \dfrac{ \partial y}{\partial \theta} \\[6pt] \dfrac{\partial z}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial z}{\partial v} & \dfrac{ \partial z}{\partial \theta} \end{vmatrix} \\ x&=uv\cos\theta \\ y&=uv\sin\theta \\ z&=\dfrac{1}{2}(v^2-u^2) \end{aligned}\] Therefore we have \[ J=\det \begin{vmatrix} v\cos\theta & u\cos\theta & -uv\sin\theta\\ v\sin\theta & u\sin\theta & uv\cos\theta\\ -u & v & 0 \end{vmatrix} \] Expanding on the third row: \[\begin{aligned} J&=-u(u^2v\cos^2\theta+u^2v\sin^2\theta) -v(uv^2\cos^2\theta+uv^2\sin^2\theta) \\ &=-u^3v-uv^3 \\ &=-uv(u^2+v^2) \end{aligned}\]

    3. Ellipsoidal:
      Here are the transformation equations and a plot of its coordinate surfaces: \[ x=at\sin\phi\cos\theta \qquad y=bt\sin\phi\sin\theta \qquad z=ct\cos\phi \]

      xxx

      \[ J=abct^2\sin\phi \]

      To find the Jacobian we need to find \[ J=\dfrac{\partial (x,y,z)}{\partial (t,\phi,\theta)} \] \[\begin{aligned} x&=at\sin\phi\cos\theta \\ y&=bt\sin\phi\sin\theta \\ z&=ct\cos\phi \end{aligned}\] Therefore we have tangent vectors: \[\begin{aligned} \vec{e}_t &=\left\langle a\sin\phi\cos\theta,b\sin\phi\sin\theta,c\cos\phi\right\rangle \\ \vec{e}_\phi &=\left\langle at\cos\phi\cos\theta,bt\cos\phi\sin\theta,-ct\sin\phi\right\rangle \\ \vec{e}_\theta &=\left\langle -at\sin\phi\sin\theta,bt\sin\phi\cos\theta,0\right\rangle \end{aligned}\] So \[\begin{aligned} J&=\det \begin{vmatrix} a\sin\phi\cos\theta & b\sin\phi\sin\theta & c\cos\phi\\ at\cos\phi\cos\theta & bt\cos\phi\sin\theta & -ct\sin\phi\\ -at\sin\phi\sin\theta & bt\sin\phi\cos\theta & 0 \end{vmatrix} \\ &=-at\sin\phi\sin\theta(-bct\sin^2\phi\sin\theta-bct\cos^2\phi\sin\theta) \\ &-bt\sin\phi\cos\theta(-act\sin^2\phi\cos\theta-act\cos^2\phi\cos\theta) \\ &=abct^2(\sin^3\phi\sin^2\theta+\sin\phi\cos^2\phi\sin^2\theta) \\ &+abct^2(\sin^3\phi\cos^2\theta+\sin\phi\cos^2\phi\cos^2\theta) \\ &=abct^2(\sin^3\phi+\sin\phi\cos^2\phi) \\ &=abct^2\sin\phi \end{aligned}\]

    To find the Jacobian, we first solve for \(x\) and \(y\). By adding and subtracting the two equations we get: \[ u+v=2x \qquad u-v=2y \] So \[ x=\dfrac{u+v}{2} \qquad y=\dfrac{u-v}{2} \] So the Jacobian is \[ J=\left| \begin{vmatrix} \dfrac{1}{2} & \dfrac{1}{2} \\[8pt] \dfrac{1}{2} & \dfrac{-1}{2} \end{vmatrix} \right| =\left|\dfrac{-1}{4}-\,\dfrac{1}{4}\right|=\dfrac{1}{2} \] To find the limits on the integral, we look at the boundary of the region.   28. Find the centroid of the quarter circle in the first quadrant of radius \(4\).

    \(\displaystyle (\bar{x},\bar{y}) =\left(\dfrac{16}{3\pi},\dfrac{16}{3\pi}\right) \approx(1.7,1.7) \)

    The area of the region is one quarter of the area of a circle of radius \(4\): \[ A=\dfrac{1}{4}\pi(4)^2=4\pi \] The \(x\)-bounds of the region are \(0\le x\le4\). To find the \(y\)-bounds, we solve \(x^2+y^2=16\) to get \(y=\sqrt{16-x^2}\). So the \(y\)-bounds are \(0\le y\le\sqrt{16-x^2}\). So \(A_x\) is: \[\begin{aligned} A_x&=\int_0^4\int_0^{\sqrt{16-x^2}} x\,dy\,dx =\int_0^4 \left[\rule{0pt}{10pt}xy\right]_{y=0}^{\sqrt{16-x^2}}\,dx \\ &=\int_0^4 x\sqrt{16-x^2}\,dx \end{aligned}\] We solve using substitution, where \(u=16-x^2\) and \(du=-2xdx\): \[\begin{aligned} A_x&=-\dfrac{1}{2}\int_{u=16}^0 u^{1/2}\,du =-\dfrac{1}{2}\left[\rule{0pt}{10pt}\dfrac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\right]_{16}^0 \\ &=--\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{128}{3}=\dfrac{64}{3} \end{aligned}\] Next, we compute \(A_y\): \[\begin{aligned} A_y&=\int_0^4\int_0^{\sqrt{16-x^2}} y\,dy\,dx =\int_0^4 \left[\rule{0pt}{10pt}\dfrac{1}{2}y^2\right]_{y=0}^{\sqrt{16-x^2}}\,dx \\ &=\int_0^4 \left(8-\dfrac{1}{2}x^2\right)\,dx =\left[\rule{0pt}{10pt}8x-\dfrac{1}{6}x^3\right]_0^4 \\ &=32-\dfrac{32}{3}=\dfrac{64}{3} \end{aligned}\] (Notice that \(A_x=A_y\) because the region is symmetrical in the \(x\) and \(y\) directions). We can now find the centroid: \[\begin{aligned} (\bar x, \bar y)&=\left(\dfrac{A_x}{A},\dfrac{A_y}{A}\right) =\left(\dfrac{64}{3}\dfrac{1}{4\pi},\dfrac{64}{3}\dfrac{1}{4\pi}\right) \\ &=\left(\dfrac{16}{3\pi},\dfrac{16}{3\pi}\right) \approx(1.7,1.7) \end{aligned}\]
