  43. Prove: If a sequence is unbounded, then it cannot have a limit.

    Theorem: If a sequence is unbounded, it cannot have a limit.
    Proof:(indirect) Assume the sequence \(\{a_n\}\) does have a limit. i.e. \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}a_n=L\).

It appears that the sequence is of the form \(\infty^0\), so we apply \(e^{\ln}\) inside the limit, move the limit inside the exponential, and then move the exponent \(\dfrac{1}{n}\) outside the log: \[\begin{aligned} \lim_{n\to\infty}(n^2+5)^{\frac{1}{n}} &=\lim_{n\to\infty}{\large e}^{\ln(n^2+5)^{1/n}} \\ &={\large e}^{\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\ln(n^2+5)} \end{aligned}\] Now \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{1}{n}\ln(n^2+5)\) is of the form \(0\cdot\infty\), so we write the \(\dfrac{1}{n}\) factor as \(n\) in the denominator and apply l'Hospital's rule: \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{1}{n}\ln(n^2+5)=\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{\ln(n^2+5)}{n} \overset{\text{l'H}}=\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{2n}{n^2+5} \] But \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{2n}{n^2+5}\) is still of the form \(\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}\)! So we have to apply l'Hospital's rule again. \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{2n}{n^2+5}\overset{\text{l'H}}=\lim_{n\to\infty}\dfrac{2}{2n} =0 \] Therefore: \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{n^2+5}=e^0=1 \]

    ad

    Determine whether each recursively defined sequence is monotonic, bounded, convergent or divergent. If convergent find the limit.

  53. \(a_1=\dfrac{1}{2} \qquad a_{n+1}=\dfrac{1}{1-a_n}\)

    Write out the first \(4\) terms of the sequence.

    The sequence is divergent. It is not monotonic but it is bounded above by \(2\) and bounded below by \(-1\).

    \(\begin{aligned} a_1&=\dfrac{1}{2} \\ a_2&=2 \\ a_3&=-1 \\ a_4&=\dfrac{1}{2} \end{aligned}\)
    Notice that \(a_4=a_1\). Therefore the sequence will oscillate like \(\dfrac{1}{2},2,-1,\dfrac{1}{2},2,-1,\dfrac{1}{2},2,-1,\ldots\) and cannot have a limit. Also the sequence is not monotonic and is bounded above by \(2\) and bounded below by \(-1\).

    ad 

  54. \(a_1=\sqrt{3} \qquad a_{n+1}=\sqrt{3+a_n}\)

    The sequence is increasing and bounded — both can be shown by mathematical induction.
    \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}\)

    \(\begin{aligned} a_1&=\sqrt{3}=1.732 \\ a_2&=\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3}}=2.175 \\ a_3&=\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3}}}=2.275 \\ a_4&=\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3}}}}=2.297 \\ a_5&=\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3+\sqrt{3}}}}}=2.301 \end{aligned}\)
    It appears that the sequence is increasing and bounded. Let us use mathematical induction to show increasing.
     1. \(a_1\lt a_2\)
     2. Assume \(a_k\le a_{k+1}\) and prove \(a_{k+1}\le a_{k+2}\). In fact: \(a_{k+2}=\sqrt{3+a_{k+1}}>\sqrt{3+a_{k}}=a_{k+1}\) and we are done.
    Thus the sequence is strictly increasing from \(a_1=\sqrt{3}\).

    To find the possible limits, we proceed as usual. Let \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}a_n=L\). Hence \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}a_{n+1}=L\) also. Taking limits of both sides of \(a_{n+1}=\sqrt{3+a_n}\) we get \(L=\sqrt{3+L}\) so that \(L^2-L-3=0\), and so \(L=\dfrac{1\pm\sqrt{13}}{2}\). Since the sequence is increasing from \(a_1=\sqrt{3}\), the only possible limit is \(L=\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}\).

    It remains to check the terms are bounded above by \(\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}\). We use mathematical induction.
     1. \(a_1=\sqrt{3}\lt\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}\)
     2. Assume \(a_k\lt\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}\) and prove \(a_{k+1}\lt\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}\).
    \(a_{k+1}=\sqrt{3+a_k}\lt\sqrt{3+\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}}=\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}\)

    Therefore \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}a_n=\dfrac{1+\sqrt{13}}{2}=2.303\).

    ad 

  55. A government project now costing \(3\) billion a year is to be reduced by \(2\dfrac{1}{2}\%\) per year beginning in \(2000\).

    1. Write an expression for the amount budgeted for the program after \(n\) years.

      \(a_n=3000000000(0.975)^n\) (\(3\) billion)

      Each year the amount budgeted will be the amount budgeted the year before times \(0.975\) (since the amount budgeted is reduced by \(2\dfrac{1}{2}\%\) a year and \(1-0.025=0.975\)). The original amount budgeted was \(3000000000\) dollars so the amount budgeted after \(n\) years is \(a_n=3000000000(0.975)^n\).

      ad 

    2. How long will it take for the program cost to be reduced by \(1\) billion?

      \(n=16.02\) — about \(16\) years.

      From part (a) we found the amount budgeted after \(n\) years is \(3000000000(.975)^n\). We solve for \(n\) when the amount budgeted is equal to \(2\) billion (since \(3\) billion minus \(1\) billion is equal to \(2\) billion): \[\begin{aligned} 3000000000(0.975)^n&=2000000000 \\ (0.975)^n&=\dfrac{2}{3} \\ n&=\log_{0.975}\left(\dfrac{2}{3}\right)=16.02 \end{aligned}\]

      ad 

    3. Does the sequence of budgeted amounts converge?

      The sequence converges.
      \(\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}a_n=0\)

      From part (a) we found the amount budgeted after \(n\) years is \(3000000000(0.975)^n\).
      Taking the limit of this: \[\begin{aligned} \lim_{n\to\infty}3000000000(0.975)^n&=3000000000\lim_{n\to\infty}(0.975)^n \\ &=3000000000\lim_{n\to\infty}e^{n\ln(0.975)} \\ &=3000000000\large e^{\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}{n\ln(0.975)}} \end{aligned}\] Notice that \(\ln(0.975)\) is negative. That means \(\lim\limits_{n\to\infty}n\ln(0.975)\) and therefore: \[ \lim_{n\to\infty}3000000000(0.975)^n=e^{-\infty}=0 \] Hence the sequence converges.
