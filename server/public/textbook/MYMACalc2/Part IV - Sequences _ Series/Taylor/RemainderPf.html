23. Taylor Series

e. Taylor Remainder

The Taylor remainder of degree \(k\) centered at \(x=a\) for the function \(f(x)\) is \[ R_k f(x)=f(x)-T_k f(x) \] where the Taylor polynomial of degree \(k\) is \[ T_k f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^k \dfrac{\,f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n \]

1. Formula and Inequality

If the \((k+1)^\text{st}\) derivative exists on an open interval containing \(a\) and \(x\), then there is a number \(c\) between \(a\) and \(x\) such that the Taylor remainder of degree \(k\) is \[ R_k f(x)=\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{(k+1)!}(x-a)^{k+1} \]

Notice that in the case when \(k=0\), the remainder is \(R_0 f(x)=f(x)-f(a)\), and so the Taylor Remainder Formula Theorem is just the Mean Value Theorem for Derivatives:

If \(f'(x)\) exists on an open interval containing \(a\) and \(x\), then there is a number \(c\) between \(a\) and \(x\) such that \[ f'(c)=\dfrac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a} \] or equivalently, \[ f(x)=f(a)+f'(c)(x-a) \]

Notice that the second version looks like the equation of the tangent line except that the derivative \(f'(c)\) is evaluated at \(c\) instead of \(a\).

The Mean Value Theorem was proved in Calculus 1 and is the basis of the following proof.

a. Proof

Since the \((k+1)^\text{st}\) derivative of \(f(x)\) exists on an open interval containing \(a\) and \(x\), \(f^{(k)}(x)\) is a function satisfying the hypotheses of the Mean Value Theorem for derivatives. So we conclude there is a number \(c\) between \(a\) and \(x\) such that \[ f^{(k)}(x)=f^{(k)}(a)+f^{(k+1)}(c)(x-a) \qquad\qquad \text{(0)} \] The antiderivative of equation \((0)\) is \[ f^{(k-1)}(x) =f^{(k)}(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{2}(x-a)^2+C_1 \]

Because \[\begin{aligned} \dfrac{d}{dx}&\left[f^{(k)}(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{2}(x-a)^2\right] \\ &=f^{(k)}(a)+f^{(k+1)}(c)(x-a) \end{aligned}\]

To find \(C_1\), set \(x=a\) to get \(C_1=f^{(k-1)}(a)\). Thus \[\begin{aligned} f^{(k-1)}(x) &=f^{(k-1)}(a)+f^{(k)}(a)(x-a) \\ &\quad+\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{2}(x-a)^2 \qquad\qquad \text{(1)} \end{aligned}\] The antiderivative of equation \((1)\) is: \[\begin{aligned} f^{(k-2)}(x) &=f^{(k-1)}(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{f^{(k)}(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 \\ &\quad+\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{3!}(x-a)^3+C_2 \end{aligned}\] To find \(C_2\), set \(x=a\) to get \(C_2=f^{(k-2)}(a)\). Thus \[\begin{aligned} f^{(k-2)}(x) &=f^{(k-2)}(a)+f^{(k-1)}(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{f^{(k)}(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 \\ &\quad+\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{3!}(x-a)^3 \qquad\qquad \text{(2)} \end{aligned}\] Proceeding in this manner for \(k\) steps (by induction) we finally obtain \[\begin{aligned} f(x) &=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{f''(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 \\ &\quad+\cdots+\dfrac{\,f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k +\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{(k+1)!}(x-a)^{k+1} \qquad\qquad\text{(k)} \end{aligned}\] The terms up to degree \(k\) are the Taylor polynomial for \(f\) of degree \(k\), i.e. \(T_kf(x)\). So the last term is the Taylor remainder, i.e. \[ R_kf(x)=\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{(k+1)!}(x-a)^{k+1} \]

Notice that the right side of equation (k) looks like the Taylor polynomial for \(f\) of degree \(k+1\), i.e. \(T_{k+1}f(x)\), except that the derivative \(f^{(k+1)}(c)\) is evaluated at \(c\) instead of \(a\).
