Higher Derivatives

  1. Find the first, second and third derivatives of \(f(x)=3x^4+x^2+2x\).

    This is simply repeatedly taking the derivative.

    \(\begin{aligned} f'(x)&=12x^3+2x+2 \\ f''(x)&=36x^2+2 \\ f'''(x)&=72x \end{aligned}\)

    The first derivative is: \[f'(x)=12x^3+2x+2\] The second derivative is: \[f''(x)=36x^2+2\] The third derivative is: \[f'''(x)=72x\]

  2. Find the first and second derivatives of \(p(x)=\sin x+e^x+5x^3+x^2\)

    \(\begin{aligned} p'(x)&=\cos x+e^x+15x^2+2x \\ p''(x)&=-\sin x+e^x+30x+2 \end{aligned}\)

    The first derivative is: \[ p'(x)=\cos x+e^x+15x^2+2x \] And the second derivative is: \[ p''(x)=-\sin x+e^x+30x+2 \]

  3. Find \(p'(t)\) and \(p''(t)\), if \(p(t)=\dfrac{12t^7-5^t}{2t^4}\).

    \(\begin{aligned} p'(t) &=\dfrac{84t^6-\ln(5)5^t}{2t^4}-\dfrac{2(12t^7-5^t)}{t^5} \\ p''(t) &=\dfrac{504t^5-(\ln(5))^2 5^t}{2t^4} -\dfrac{4[84t^6-\ln(5)5^t]}{t^5} +\dfrac{10(12t^7-5^t)}{t^6} \end{aligned}\)

    The first derivative is: \[\begin{aligned} p'(t) &=\dfrac{2t^4\dfrac{d}{dx}(12t^7-5^t)-(12t^7-5^t)\dfrac{d}{dx}(2t^4)} {(2t^4)^2} \\ &=\dfrac{2t^4(84t^6-\ln(5)5^t)-(12t^7-5^t)8t^3}{4t^8} \\ &=\dfrac{84t^6-\ln(5)5^t}{2t^4}-\dfrac{2(12t^7-5^t)}{t^5} \end{aligned}\] The second derivative is: \[\begin{aligned} p''(t) &=\dfrac{2t^4\dfrac{d}{dx}[84t^6-\ln(5)5^t]-[84t^6-\ln(5)5^t]\dfrac{d}{dx}(2t^4)} {(2t^4)^2} \\ &\quad-\dfrac{t^5\dfrac{d}{dx}[2(12t^7-5^t)]-2(12t^7-5^t)\dfrac{d}{dx}(t^5)} {(t^5)^2} \\ &=\dfrac{2t^4[504t^5-(\ln(5))^2 5^t]-[84t^6-\ln(5)5^t](8t^3)}{4t^8} \\ &\quad-\dfrac{t^5[2(84t^6-\ln(5)5^t)]-2(12t^7-5^t)(5t^4)}{t^{10}} \\ &=\dfrac{504t^5-(\ln(5))^2 5^t}{2t^4} -\dfrac{2[84t^6-\ln(5)5^t]}{t^5} \\ &\quad-\dfrac{2(84t^6-\ln(5)5^t)}{t^5} +\dfrac{10(12t^7-5^t)}{t^6} \\ &=\dfrac{504t^5-(\ln(5))^2 5^t}{2t^4} -\dfrac{4[84t^6-\ln(5)5^t]}{t^5} +\dfrac{10(12t^7-5^t)}{t^6} \\ \end{aligned}\]

  4. Find the \(71^\text{st}\) derivative of \(f(x)=\sin x\)

    The fourth derivative of \(\sin x\) is \(\sin x\)

    \(f^{[71]}(x)=-\cos x\)

    Since \[\begin{aligned} f'(x)=\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin x&=\cos x &f'''(x)=\dfrac{d}{dx}(-\sin x)&=-\cos x \\ f''(x)=\dfrac{d}{dx}\cos x&=-\sin x \qquad &f^{[4]}(x)=\dfrac{d}{dx}(-\cos x)&=\sin x \end{aligned}\] the higher derivatives of \(\sin x\) repeat every \(4^\text{th}\) derivative. Since \(68\) is a multiple of \(4\), we conclude \(f^{[68]}(x)=\sin x\) and: \[f^{[71]}(x)=-\cos x\]

    ls 

    Some of you may recognize how we used the repetition of the higher derivatives of \(\sin x\) as modular or clock arithmetic, where: \[\begin{aligned} 1\equiv1\mod 4 \qquad \qquad &2\equiv2\mod 4 \\ 3\equiv3\mod 4 \qquad \qquad &4\equiv0\mod 4 \\ 5\equiv1\mod 4 \qquad \qquad &6\equiv2\mod 4 \\ \qquad \vdots \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad &\qquad \vdots \\ 71\equiv3\mod 4 \qquad \qquad &72\equiv0\mod 4 \end{aligned}\]

  5. Consider the function \(g(x)=x^6-30x^4\). Find:
    1. the interval(s) on which \(g\) is increasing.
    2. the interval(s) on which \(g\) is decreasing.

    3. all local minima and maxima of \(g\).

      The local minima and maxima are the points where \(g(x)\) changes from increasing to decreasing and vice-versa.

      \(g(x)\) is decreasing on \(\left(-\infty,-\sqrt{20}\right]\) and \(\left[0,\sqrt{20}\right]\) and is increasing on \(\left[-\sqrt{20},0\right]\) and \(\left[\sqrt{20},\infty\right)\). There is a local maximum at \(x=0\) and local minima at \(x=-\sqrt{20}\) and \(x=\sqrt{20}\).

      We first find and factor the derivative: \[ g'(x)=6x^5-120x^3=6x^3(x^2-20) \] The critical points are where \(g'(x)=0\), i.e. \(x=0,\sqrt{20},-\sqrt{20}\). We now test a point in each interval to see if \(g'(x)=6x^3(x^2-20)\) is positive or negative in that interval. \[\begin{aligned} &g'(-5)=6(-125)(25-20)=-3750 \lt 0 \\ &g'(-1)=6(-1)(1-20)=114 \gt 0 \\ &g'(1)=6(1)(1-20)=-114 \lt 0 \\ &g'(5)=6(125)(25-20)=3750 \gt 0 \end{aligned}\] We have marked these on the number line: So \(g(x)\) is decreasing on \(\left(-\infty,-\sqrt{20}\right]\) and \(\left[0,\sqrt{20}\right]\) and is increasing on \(\left[-\sqrt{20},0\right]\) and \(\left[\sqrt{20},\infty\right)\). So the graph has a local maximum at \(x=0\) and local minima at \(x=-\sqrt{20}\) and \(x=\sqrt{20}\).

      ls 

    4. the interval(s) on which \(g\) is concave up.
    5. the interval(s) on which \(g\) is concave down.

    6. the inflection point(s) of \(g\).

      Factor the second derivative and determine where it is positive and negative.

      \(g(x)\) is concave up for \((-\infty,-\sqrt{12}]\) and \([\sqrt{12},\infty)\) and is concave down for \([-\sqrt{12},\sqrt{12}]\). The inflection points are \(x=-\sqrt{12}\), and \(x=\sqrt{12}\).

      The first derivative was \(g'(x)=6x^5-120x^3\). So the second derivative is: \[ g''(x)=30x^4-360x^2=30x^2(x^2-12) \] The potential inflection points are where \(g''(x)=0\), i.e. \(x=0,\sqrt{12},-\sqrt{12}\). We now test a point in each interval to see if \(g''(x)=30x^2(x^2-12)\) is positive or negative in that interval: \[\begin{aligned} &g''(-4)=30(16)(16-12)=1920 \gt 0 \\ &g''(-1)=30(1)(1-12)=-330 \lt 0 \\ &g''(1)=30(1)(1-12)=-330 \lt 0 \\ &g''(4)=30(16)(16-12)=1920 \gt 0 \end{aligned}\] We have marked the signs on the number line: So, \(g(x)\) is concave up for \((-\infty,-\sqrt{12}]\) and \([\sqrt{12},\infty)\) and is concave down for \([-\sqrt{12},\sqrt{12}]\). There are inflection points at \(x=-\sqrt{12}\), and \(x=\sqrt{12}\). Note: \(x=0\) is NOT an inflection point since the concavity did not change.

      ls 

      We have found the function is increasing on the intervals \(\left[-\sqrt{20},0\right]\) and \(\left[\sqrt{20},\infty\right)\), and decreasing on \(\left(-\infty,-\sqrt{20}\right]\) and \(\left[0,\sqrt{20}\right]\). Further it is concave up on the intervals \((-\infty,-\sqrt{12}]\) and \([\sqrt{12},\infty)\) and concave down on \([-\sqrt{12},\sqrt{12}]\). In addition there are local minima at \(\pm \sqrt{20}\), a local maximum at \(0\), and inflection points at \(\pm \sqrt{12}\). All of this can be seen in the following graph of our function \(g(x)=x^6-30x^4\).

  6. Find the point(s) on the graph of the function \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^6}{120}-\dfrac{x^4}{24}\) where the concavity (second derivative) has a local minimum or local maximum and find the global minimum and maximum if they exist.

    The concavity is \(f''(x)\) which has extrema when \(f'''(x)=0\).

    The local minima of the concavity are \(x=-1\) and \(x=1\) and the local maxima is \(x=0\).
    The global minima are \(x=-1\) and \(x=1\) but there is no global maximum.

    The first derivative is: \[ f'(x)=\dfrac{x^5}{20}-\dfrac{x^3}{6} \] The concavity is the second derivative: \[ f''(x)=\dfrac{x^4}{4}-\dfrac{x^2}{2} \] To extremize the concavity, we set its derivative equal to \(0\) and solve for any critical points. However, the derivative of the concavity is the third derivative of \(f\): \[\begin{aligned} f'''(x)=x^3-x&=0 \\ x(x-1)(x+1)&=0 \end{aligned}\] So the critical points are: \(-1\), \(0\) and \(1\). To find out which ones are local minima or maxima, we apply the second derivative test. (If the second derivative is positive, the critical point is a local minimum. If the second derivative is negative, the critical point is a local maximum.) Here, the second derivative of the concavity is the fourth derivative of \(f\): \[ f^{(4)}(x)=3x^2-1 \] So: \[ f^{(4)}(-1)=2 \gt 0 \qquad f^{(4)}(0)=-1 \lt 0 \qquad f^{(4)}(1)=2 \gt 0 \] Hence, \(x=-1\) and \(x=1\) are local minima of the concavity while \(x=0\) is a local maximum.

    A plot of the concavity, \(f''(x)=\dfrac{x^4}{4}-\dfrac{x^2}{2}\), shows that \(x=-1\) and \(x=1\) are global minima but there is no global maximum.

  7. Find the concavity of the general parabola \(y=ax^2+bx+c\) where \(a\), \(b\) and \(c\) are constants.

    The concavity is the second derivative.

    \(y''=2a\)

    The first derivative is: \[y'=2ax+b\] Then the concavity, which is the second derivative, is: \[y''=2a\]

  10. If the position is \(x=3t^4-t^3+5t^2\), find the velocity, acceleration and jerk.

    Velocity is the derivative of the position, acceleration is the derivative of the velocity, and jerk is the derivative of the acceleration.

    \(\begin{aligned} v&=x'=12t^3-3t^2+10t \\ a&=x''=36t^2-6t+10 \\ j&=x'''=72t-6 \end{aligned}\)

    Velocity is the derivative of postion: \[ v=x'=12t^3-3t^2+10t \] Acceleration is the derivative of velocity: \[ a=x''=36t^2-6t+10 \] Jerk is the derivative of acceleration: \[ j=x'''=72t-6 \]

  11. The position of a harmonic oscillator is \(y=5\sin(2t+4)\), find its velocity, acceleration and jerk.

    Velocity is the derivative of the position, acceleration is the derivative of the velocity, and jerk is the derivative of the acceleration.

    \(\begin{aligned} v&=y'=10\cos(2t+4) \\ a&=y''=-20\sin(2t+4) \\ j&=y'''=-40\cos(2t+4) \end{aligned}\)

    Velocity is the derivative of postion: \[ v=y'=5\cos(2t+4)(2)=10\cos(2t+4) \qquad \] Acceleration is the derivative of velocity: \[ a=y''=-10\sin(2t+4)(2)=-20\sin(2t+4) \] Jerk is the derivative of acceleration: \[ j=y'''=-20\cos(2t+4)(2)=-40\cos(2t+4) \]

  12. The position of any harmonic oscillator is \(z=A\sin\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right)\), where \(A\) is the amplitude, \(P\) is the period and \(\phi\) is the phase shift. Find its velocity, acceleration and jerk.
    Note: \(A\), \(P\) and \(\phi\) are constants.

    Since \(A\), \(P\) and \(\phi\) are constants, they don't get differentiated.

    \(\begin{aligned} v&=z'=\dfrac{2\pi A}{P}\cos\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \\ a&=z''=-\,\dfrac{4\pi^2 A}{P^2}\sin\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \\ j&=z'''=-\,\dfrac{8\pi^3 A}{P^3}\cos\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \end{aligned}\)

    Velocity is the derivative of the position \(z\): \[\begin{aligned} v=z'&=A\dfrac{d}{dt}\sin\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \\ &=A\cos\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right)\dfrac{2\pi}{P} \\ &=\dfrac{2\pi A}{P}\cos\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \\ \end{aligned}\] Acceleration is the derivative of the velocity \(z'\): \[\begin{aligned} a=z''&=\dfrac{2\pi A}{P}\dfrac{d}{dt}\cos\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \\ &=-\,\dfrac{2\pi A}{P}\sin\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right)\dfrac{2\pi}{P} \\ &=-\,\dfrac{4\pi^2 A}{P^2}\sin\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \\ \end{aligned}\] Jerk is the derivative of the acceleration \(z''\): \[\begin{aligned} j=z'''&=-\,\dfrac{4\pi^2 A}{P^2}\dfrac{d}{dt}\sin\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \\ &=-\,\dfrac{4\pi^2 A}{P^2}\cos\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right)\dfrac{2\pi}{P} \\ &=-\,\dfrac{8\pi^3 A}{P^3}\cos\left(\dfrac{2\pi t-\phi}{P}\right) \end{aligned}\]

  14. Approximate \(\sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{8.3}\) by using the linear, quadratic and cubic approximations to \(f(x)=\sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{x}\) centered at \(x=8\). How many decimal places of accuracy do you get from each approzimation, given that \(\sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{8.3}\approx2.0246938520054570739\)?

    1. Linear approximation.

      The linear approximation at \(x=a\) is: \[ f(x)\approx f_{\tan}(x)\equiv f(a)+f'(a)(x-a) \] So the linear approximation at \(x=8\) is: \[ f(x)\approx f_{\tan}(x)\equiv f(8)+f'(8)(x-8) \] So you need to compute \(f(8)\) and \(f'(8)\).

      Using the linear approximation we get \[ \sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{8.3}=f(8.3)\approx f_{\tan}(8.3)=2.025 \] which agrees with the exact value to \(4\) digits after rounding.

      To find the linear approximation, we need the function and its derivative at \(x=8\): \[\begin{aligned} f(x)&=x^{1/3} \quad &f(8)&=8^{1/3}=2 \\ f'(x)&=\dfrac{1}{3}x^{-2/3}=\dfrac{1}{3x^{2/3}} \quad &f'(8)&=\dfrac{1}{3\cdot8^{2/3}}=\dfrac{1}{12} \end{aligned}\] Then linear approximation is: \[\begin{aligned} f(x)\approx f_{\tan}(x)&\equiv f(8)+f'(8)(x-8) \\ &=2+\dfrac{1}{12}(x-8) \end{aligned}\] Consequently: \[\begin{aligned} \sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{8.3}&=f(8.3)\approx f_{\tan}(8.3) \\ &=2+\dfrac{1}{12}(8.3-8) \\ &=2+.025=2.025 \end{aligned}\] This agrees with the exact value to \(4\) digits after rounding.

    2. Quadratic approximation.

      The quadratic approximation at \(x=a\) is: \[\begin{aligned} f(x)&\approx f_\text{quad}(x) \\ &\equiv f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{1}{2}f''(a)(x-a)^2 \end{aligned}\] So the quadratic approximation at \(x=8\) is: \[\begin{aligned} f(x)&\approx f_\text{quad}(x) \\ &\equiv f(8)+f'(8)(x-8)+\dfrac{1}{2}f''(8)(x-8)^2 \end{aligned}\] So you need to compute \(f''(8)\) in addition to what you used for the linear approximation.

      Using the quadratic approximation we get \[ \sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{8.3} =f(8.3)\approx f_{\text{quad}}(8.3)=2.0246875 \] which agrees with the exact value to \(6\) digits after rounding.

      To find the quadratic approximation, we also need the second derivative at \(x=8\): \[\begin{aligned} f''(x)&=-\,\dfrac{2}{9}x^{-5/3}=-\,\dfrac{2}{9x^{5/3}} \\ f''(8)&=-\,\dfrac{2}{9\cdot8^{5/3}}=-\,\dfrac{1}{144} \end{aligned}\] Then quadratic approximation is: \[\begin{aligned} f(x)\approx f_{\text{quad}}(x)&\equiv f(8)+f'(8)(x-8)+\dfrac{1}{2}f''(8)(x-8)^2 \\ &=2+\dfrac{1}{12}(x-8)-\dfrac{1}{288}(x-8)^2 \end{aligned}\] Consequently: \[\begin{aligned} \sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{8.3} &=f(8.3)\approx f_{\text{quad}}(8.3) \\ &=2+\dfrac{1}{12}(8.3-8)-\dfrac{1}{288}(8.3-8)^2 \\ &=2+.025-.0003125=2.0246875 \end{aligned}\] This agrees with the exact value to \(6\) digits after rounding.

    3. Cubic approximation.

      The cubic approximation at \(x=a\) is: \[\begin{aligned} f(x)&\approx f_\text{cubic}(x) \\ &\equiv f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{1}{2}f''(a)(x-a)^2 \\ &\quad+\dfrac{1}{6}f'''(a)(x-a)^3 \end{aligned}\] So the cubic approximation at \(x=8\) is: \[\begin{aligned} f(x)&\approx f_\text{cubic}(x) \\ &\equiv f(8)+f'(8)(x-8)+\dfrac{1}{2}f''(8)(x-8)^2 \\ &\quad+\dfrac{1}{6}f'''(8)(x-8)^3 \end{aligned}\] So you need to compute \(f'''(8)\) in addition to what you used for the quadratic approximation.

      Using the cubic approximation we get \[ \sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{8.3} =f(8.3)\approx f_{\text{cubic}}(8.3)=2.024\,694\,010\,416\,\bar6 \] which agrees with the exact value to \(7\) digits after rounding.

      To find the cubic approximation, we also need the third derivative at \(x=8\): \[\begin{aligned} f'''(x)&=\dfrac{10}{27}x^{-8/3}=\dfrac{10}{27x^{8/3}} \\ f'''(8)&=\dfrac{10}{27\cdot8^{8/3}}=\dfrac{5}{3456} \end{aligned}\] Then cubic approximation is: \[\begin{aligned} f(x)&\approx f_{\text{cubic}}(x) \\ &\equiv f(8)+f'(8)(x-8)+\dfrac{1}{2}f''(8)(x-8)^2+\dfrac{1}{6}f'''(8)(x-8)^3 \\ &=2+\dfrac{1}{12}(x-8)-\dfrac{1}{288}(x-8)^2+\dfrac{5}{6\cdot3456}(x-8)^3 \end{aligned}\] Consequently: \[\begin{aligned} \sqrt[\scriptsize 3]{8.3} &=f(8.3)\approx f_{\text{cubic}}(8.3) \\ &=2+\dfrac{1}{12}(8.3-8)-\dfrac{1}{288}(8.3-8)^2+\dfrac{5}{6\cdot3456}(8.3-8)^3 \\ &=2+.025-.000\,3125+.000\,006\,510\,416\,\bar6 \\ &=2.024\,694\,010\,416\,\bar6 \end{aligned}\] This agrees with the exact value to \(7\) digits after rounding.
