23. Taylor Series

e. Taylor Remainder

When we approximate a power series, \(\displaystyle S=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n\), by one of its partial sums \(\displaystyle S_k=\sum_{n=0}^k a_n\), the error in the approximation, \(\displaystyle E_k=S-S_k=\sum_{n=k+1}^\infty a_n\), may be bounded by an integral bound, a comparison bound or an alternating series bound, as appropriate. When we approximate a function by a Taylor polynomial, the error in the approximation is called the Taylor remainder.

The Taylor remainder of degree \(k\) centered at \(x=a\) for the function \(f(x)\) is \[ R_k f(x)=f(x)-T_k f(x) \] where the Taylor polynomial of degree \(k\) is \[ T_k f(x)=\sum_{n=0}^k \dfrac{\,f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n \]

For values of \(x\) for which the Alternating Series Test, the Integral Test or the Simple Comparison test are sufficient to prove convergence, the corresponding bounds may be applied to the remainder. (This was done in the example on the previous page.) However, when these techniques fail, the Taylor Remainder Inequality (given below) will give a bound on the error in the Taylor polynomial approximation. (See the example and exercise on the next page.) Further, the Taylor Remainder Inequality will finally allow us to determine whether the Taylor series for a function \(f(x)\) actually converges to the function \(f(x)\). (See the example and exercise on the following page.) This inequality is based on the Taylor Remainder Formula given in the next theorem.

1. Formula and Inequality

The above definition says what the remainder is, but it does not give a formula for it or even put an upper bound on it. The following theorem gives a formula and the next one says how to put an upper bound on it.

If the \((k+1)^\text{st}\) derivative exists on an open interval containing \(a\) and \(x\), then there is a number \(c\) between \(a\) and \(x\) such that the Taylor remainder of degree \(k\) is \[ R_k f(x)=\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{(k+1)!}(x-a)^{k+1} \]     The proof is straight forward and based on the Mean Value Theorem. Try reading it.

Using the definition of the remainder, the Taylor remainder may be combined with the Taylor polynomial to say: \[\begin{aligned} f(x) &=T_k f(x)+R_k f(x) \\ &=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)+\dfrac{f''(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 \\ &\quad+\cdots+\dfrac{\,f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k +\dfrac{f^{(k+1)}(c)}{(k+1)!}(x-a)^{k+1} \end{aligned}\] So the remainder is just the next term in the Taylor series except that the \((k+1)^\text{st}\) derivative is evaluated at \(c\) instead of \(a\).

For \(k=0\), Taylor's Theorem says there is a \(c\) for which \[ f(x)=f(a)+f'(c)(x-a) \] This is just the Mean Value Theorem for Derivatives. So Taylor's Remainder Theorem is often called the Extended Mean Value Theorem. In fact, the proof of Taylor's Theorem is based on the ordinary Mean Value Theorem.

The problem with this theorem is that there is no way to determine the value of \(c\) guaranteed by the theorem. So instead, we find a bound on \( f^{(k+1)}(c)\) and use it to put a bound on the remainder \(R_k f(x)\):

If \(a\) is in an open interval \(I\) and \(M \ge |f^{(k+1)}(c)|\) for all \(c\) in the interval \(I\), then the Taylor remainder of degree \(k\) centered at \(x=a\) satisfies \[ |R_k f(x)| \le \dfrac{M}{(k+1)!}|x-a|^{k+1} \] for all \(x\) in the interval \(I\). The quantity on the right is called the Taylor bound on the remainder.

On the next page we will see how the Taylor Remainder Inequality may be used to bound the error in a Taylor polynomial approximation. Then on the following page we will see how it can be used to determine when the Taylor series for a function \(f(x)\) actually converges to the function \(f(x)\).
